# Economics: Fundamentals — Tutor Reference

## Core Concepts

**Demand**: The quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to purchase at various prices, ceteris paribus (all else equal). The law of demand: as price increases, quantity demanded decreases (inverse relationship). The demand curve slopes downward.

**Supply**: The quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to offer at various prices, ceteris paribus. The law of supply: as price increases, quantity supplied increases (direct relationship). The supply curve slopes upward.

**Equilibrium**: The point where the supply and demand curves intersect. At equilibrium price (P*), quantity demanded equals quantity supplied (Q*). The market naturally tends toward equilibrium.

**Surplus (excess supply)**: Occurs when price is ABOVE equilibrium. Quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded. Producers cannot sell all their goods, so price tends to fall toward equilibrium.

**Shortage (excess demand)**: Occurs when price is BELOW equilibrium. Quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied. Consumers cannot buy all they want, so price tends to rise toward equilibrium.

**Price elasticity of demand**: A measure of how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price. Elastic demand (|E| > 1): quantity changes proportionally more than price. Inelastic demand (|E| < 1): quantity changes proportionally less than price. Unit elastic (|E| = 1): proportional change is equal.

**Formula**: Price elasticity of demand = (% change in quantity demanded) / (% change in price). The result is typically negative (law of demand) but is often expressed as an absolute value.

**Determinants of elasticity**: Availability of substitutes (more substitutes = more elastic), necessity vs luxury (necessities are inelastic), time horizon (more elastic in the long run), proportion of income spent (larger share = more elastic).

## Key Distinctions

**Movement along a curve vs shift of a curve**: This is the most critical conceptual distinction in introductory economics.

- **Movement along**: Caused by a change in the good's OWN price. You slide along the existing curve. A price increase causes movement up along the demand curve (quantity demanded falls).
- **Shift of the curve**: Caused by a change in a NON-PRICE determinant. The entire curve moves left or right.
  - Demand shifters: income, tastes/preferences, price of related goods (substitutes/complements), expectations, number of buyers.
  - Supply shifters: input costs, technology, expectations, number of sellers, government policy (taxes/subsidies).

**Normal vs inferior goods**: For normal goods, demand increases when income rises. For inferior goods, demand decreases when income rises (consumers switch to preferred alternatives).

**Substitutes vs complements**: Substitutes: goods that can replace each other (Coke/Pepsi). When the price of one rises, demand for the other increases. Complements: goods used together (hot dogs/buns). When the price of one rises, demand for the other decreases.

## Common Student Misconceptions and Errors

1. **Confusing movement along a curve with a shift of the curve**: The most frequent and fundamental error in introductory economics. Students say "demand increased" when they mean "quantity demanded increased" due to a price change. A change in the good's own price causes movement along the curve. A change in income, tastes, or related goods' prices shifts the entire curve.

2. **Reversing supply and demand curve directions**: Students draw the demand curve sloping upward or the supply curve sloping downward. Demand slopes down (higher price = less quantity demanded). Supply slopes up (higher price = more quantity supplied).

3. **Confusing surplus and shortage**: Students mix up which occurs when price is above versus below equilibrium. Memory aid: surplus = price too high, producers have excess; shortage = price too low, consumers want more than is available.

4. **Thinking equilibrium means "good" or "fair"**: Students assume the equilibrium price is the "correct" or "fair" price. Equilibrium is simply where supply equals demand — it says nothing about fairness, affordability, or social desirability.

5. **Misinterpreting elasticity**: Students think elasticity is the same as slope. Elasticity is a percentage-change measure and varies along a linear demand curve. A steep curve is not necessarily inelastic, and a flat curve is not necessarily elastic (it depends on the price-quantity level).

6. **Confusing a change in demand with a change in quantity demanded**: "Demand" refers to the entire schedule/curve. "Quantity demanded" is a specific point on the curve. Saying "demand changed" means the curve shifted. Saying "quantity demanded changed" means you moved along the curve.

7. **Applying supply and demand backward**: Students think a decrease in supply (shift left) means the price will decrease. Actually, a decrease in supply raises the price (less available at every price level).

8. **Ignoring ceteris paribus**: Students change multiple variables simultaneously when analyzing a shift, making the outcome ambiguous. Proper analysis changes one variable at a time.

9. **Thinking price floors cause shortages and price ceilings cause surpluses**: It is the opposite. Price floors (above equilibrium) cause surpluses. Price ceilings (below equilibrium) cause shortages.

10. **Conflating demand and want**: Students think demand is just about desire. Economic demand requires both willingness AND ability to pay at a given price.

## Diagnostic Questions

- "Did the price of the good itself change, or did something else change? How does that affect whether the curve shifts or you move along it?" (surfaces the critical movement vs shift confusion)
- "In which direction does the demand curve slope, and why?" (surfaces curve direction errors)
- "If the price is above equilibrium, is there a surplus or a shortage? What happens next?" (surfaces surplus/shortage confusion)
- "When you say 'demand increased,' do you mean the whole curve shifted right, or that consumers bought more because the price dropped?" (surfaces demand vs quantity demanded confusion)
- "If the government sets a price ceiling below equilibrium, what happens to the quantity supplied and demanded?" (surfaces price control understanding)
- "What non-price factors could shift the demand curve for this good?" (surfaces understanding of determinants)
- "Is a steep demand curve always inelastic?" (surfaces elasticity vs slope confusion)

## Worked Example: Common Error and Correct Approach

**Problem**: The price of coffee increases. What happens in the market for tea?

**Common incorrect approach**:
"When coffee prices go up, people demand less coffee, so the demand for coffee decreases and the demand curve for coffee shifts left."
Errors: (1) The student shifted the coffee demand curve when they should have described movement along it. A price increase for coffee causes movement along the coffee demand curve (quantity demanded falls), not a shift. (2) The student never addressed the tea market.

**Correct approach**:
1. Coffee and tea are substitutes (consumers can switch between them).
2. When the price of coffee increases, consumers move along the coffee demand curve — quantity of coffee demanded falls.
3. Some consumers switch from coffee to tea at every price level.
4. This is a change in a non-price determinant for tea (price of a related good), so the demand CURVE for tea shifts RIGHT (increases).
5. With increased demand for tea, the new equilibrium has a higher price and higher quantity for tea.

**Tutoring note**: The movement-vs-shift distinction is best taught by asking two questions: (1) "What changed — the price of the good itself, or something else?" (2) "Are we talking about the market for the good whose price changed, or a different market?" If the good's own price changed, it is movement along the curve. If something else changed, the curve shifts. Using this two-question framework consistently prevents the most common error in introductory economics.
