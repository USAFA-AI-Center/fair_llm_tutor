# Chemistry: Balancing Equations — Tutor Reference

## Core Concepts

**Law of conservation of mass**: Matter cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction. The number of atoms of each element must be the same on both sides of the equation. This is the fundamental principle behind balancing.

**Chemical equation**: A symbolic representation of a chemical reaction. Reactants on the left, products on the right, separated by an arrow. Example: 2H2 + O2 -> 2H2O.

**Coefficients**: Numbers placed BEFORE chemical formulas that indicate the number of molecules (or moles) of each substance. Coefficients are the ONLY numbers you may change when balancing. Example: the "2" in 2H2O means two molecules of water.

**Subscripts**: Numbers within a chemical formula that indicate the number of atoms of an element in one molecule. Subscripts are FIXED by the identity of the compound and must NEVER be changed when balancing. Example: the "2" in H2O means each water molecule contains two hydrogen atoms.

**Oxidation states**: The charge an atom would have if all bonds were ionic. Useful for balancing redox reactions. Rules: free elements are 0; oxygen is usually -2; hydrogen is usually +1; the sum of oxidation states equals the charge on the species.

**Stoichiometry**: The quantitative relationship between reactants and products in a balanced equation. Coefficients give mole ratios.

## Key Procedures

**Balancing by inspection (trial and error)**:
1. Write the unbalanced equation.
2. List all elements present.
3. Count atoms of each element on both sides.
4. Adjust coefficients (starting with the most complex molecule or the element appearing in the fewest formulas).
5. Balance metals first, then nonmetals, then hydrogen, then oxygen last.
6. Verify all elements are balanced. Reduce coefficients to smallest whole numbers.

**Balancing redox reactions (half-reaction method)**:
1. Split into oxidation and reduction half-reactions.
2. Balance atoms other than O and H.
3. Balance O with H2O, H with H+.
4. Balance charge with electrons.
5. Multiply half-reactions so electrons cancel.
6. Add half-reactions and simplify.

**Polyatomic ions**: If a polyatomic ion (SO4^2-, NO3^-, PO4^3-) appears intact on both sides, treat it as a single unit when counting.

## Common Student Misconceptions and Errors

1. **Changing subscripts instead of coefficients**: The single most common and serious balancing error. Students change H2O to H3O or CO2 to CO3 to make atoms balance. This changes the identity of the compound entirely. H2O is water; H3O is a different species. Only coefficients may be adjusted.

2. **Forgetting polyatomic ions stay together**: Students break apart polyatomic ions and balance individual atoms, making the process much harder. Example: in Ca(OH)2 + HCl -> CaCl2 + H2O, students should treat OH as a unit, not separately balance O and H.

3. **Not balancing all elements**: Students balance one or two elements and declare the equation balanced without checking every element. They must verify every element individually.

4. **Leaving coefficients as fractions**: Students write 1/2 O2 instead of multiplying all coefficients by 2 to get whole numbers. While fractionally balanced equations are technically correct, convention requires whole-number coefficients.

5. **Balancing charge but not atoms (in ionic equations)**: Students focus on making charges balance but forget to verify that atom counts also balance, or vice versa.

6. **Counting atoms incorrectly with parentheses**: In Ca3(PO4)2, students fail to distribute the subscript 2 across the parentheses. This compound contains 3 Ca, 2 P, and 8 O atoms — not 2 P and 4 O.

7. **Ignoring state symbols**: Students do not recognize the significance of (s), (l), (g), (aq) or omit them, which can matter for net ionic equations.

8. **Starting with the wrong element**: Students try to balance oxygen or hydrogen first (which appear in many compounds), leading to endless adjustment cycles. Strategy: start with elements that appear in the fewest formulas or with the most complex molecule.

9. **Not reducing to lowest terms**: Students write 4H2 + 2O2 -> 4H2O instead of 2H2 + O2 -> 2H2O.

10. **Confusing coefficients and subscripts in atom counting**: In 2H2O, the total hydrogen count is 2 (coefficient) x 2 (subscript) = 4, not 2 or 22. Students frequently miscount when both coefficients and subscripts are present.

## Diagnostic Questions

- "When you changed that number, did you change a coefficient or a subscript? What's the difference?" (surfaces the most critical error)
- "Can you count the number of each type of atom on the left side and the right side?" (surfaces counting errors)
- "Does SO4 appear as a complete unit on both sides? Can you balance it as one piece?" (surfaces polyatomic ion handling)
- "In the formula 3Ca(PO4)2, how many phosphorus atoms are there total?" (surfaces parentheses counting)
- "Have you checked EVERY element, not just the ones you adjusted?" (surfaces incomplete verification)
- "What does the law of conservation of mass require about your balanced equation?" (surfaces conceptual understanding)
- "Can all your coefficients be divided by a common factor?" (surfaces lowest-terms issue)

## Worked Example: Common Error and Correct Approach

**Problem**: Balance the equation: Fe + O2 -> Fe2O3

**Common incorrect approach**:
Student changes the formula to Fe + O2 -> FeO3 to make iron "balance."
Error: Changed a subscript (Fe2O3 to FeO3), altering the compound. Fe2O3 is iron(III) oxide; FeO3 does not exist as a standard compound.

**Correct approach**:
1. Unbalanced: Fe + O2 -> Fe2O3
2. Count atoms: Left: 1 Fe, 2 O. Right: 2 Fe, 3 O. Neither balanced.
3. Balance Fe: put coefficient 2 on Fe. 2Fe + O2 -> Fe2O3. Now Fe: 2=2. O: 2 vs 3, not balanced.
4. Balance O: need 3 O on left. Use 3/2 as coefficient: 2Fe + 3/2 O2 -> Fe2O3.
5. Eliminate fraction by multiplying everything by 2: 4Fe + 3O2 -> 2Fe2O3.
6. Verify: Fe: 4 = 2x2 = 4. O: 3x2 = 6 = 2x3 = 6. Balanced.

**Tutoring note**: If a student changes a subscript, immediately stop and clarify the distinction between coefficients and subscripts. Use an analogy: "The subscript is like the recipe for the molecule — you cannot change it without making a different substance. The coefficient is how many copies of that molecule you have." Have them physically circle the coefficients and underline the subscripts to build visual awareness.
