# History: Historical Analysis and French Revolution — Tutor Reference

## Core Concepts

**Historical analysis**: The practice of examining primary and secondary sources to construct interpretations of past events. History is not simply memorizing dates — it involves evaluating evidence, understanding causation, considering perspective, and constructing arguments.

**Primary sources**: Documents, artifacts, or records created during the time period under study. Examples: letters, diaries, government records, photographs, speeches. Must be evaluated for bias, context, and reliability.

**Secondary sources**: Interpretations and analyses created after the fact by historians. Examples: textbooks, scholarly articles, documentaries. Built upon primary source evidence.

**Causation**: The relationship between events where one event (cause) leads to another (effect). Historical causation is typically multi-causal: events have multiple contributing causes, both long-term (structural) and short-term (triggers).

**Correlation vs causation in history**: Two events occurring near each other in time does not mean one caused the other. Historians must establish a causal mechanism, not just temporal proximity.

**Periodization**: Dividing history into distinct periods. The boundaries are always somewhat arbitrary and reflect the historian's interpretive framework.

**Historiography**: The study of how history has been written and interpreted over time. Different historians can interpret the same events differently based on their perspective, methodology, and available evidence.

## French Revolution: Key Facts

**Key dates**:
- 1789: Storming of the Bastille (July 14), Declaration of the Rights of Man (August 26), Women's March on Versailles (October 5-6)
- 1791: Constitution adopted, creating constitutional monarchy
- 1792: France declared a republic (September 22), war with Austria and Prussia
- 1793-1794: Reign of Terror under the Committee of Public Safety (Robespierre)
- 1794: Thermidorian Reaction (July 27), fall of Robespierre
- 1799: Napoleon's coup d'etat (November 9), end of the revolutionary period

**Causes (multi-causal analysis)**:
- Long-term: Feudal inequality (Three Estates system), Enlightenment ideas (Rousseau, Voltaire, Montesquieu), financial crisis from wars and royal extravagance, poor harvests and bread prices
- Medium-term: American Revolution as inspiration, Assembly of Notables failure (1787), Estates-General convened (1789)
- Immediate trigger: Third Estate locked out of meeting hall, formation of National Assembly, Tennis Court Oath

**Effects**: Abolition of feudalism, Declaration of Rights of Man, rise of nationalism, spread of revolutionary ideas across Europe, eventual rise of Napoleon, long-term influence on democratic movements worldwide.

## Common Student Misconceptions and Errors

1. **Confusing correlation with causation**: Students see two events close in time and assume one caused the other without establishing a causal mechanism. Example: "The Enlightenment happened before the Revolution, so the Enlightenment caused the Revolution." While Enlightenment ideas were a contributing factor, the causal relationship is more nuanced and involves many mediating factors.

2. **Mono-causal thinking**: Students attribute complex events to a single cause. Example: "The French Revolution happened because people were hungry." Hunger was one factor, but the revolution resulted from the intersection of economic, social, political, and intellectual causes.

3. **Wrong dates and chronological confusion**: Students mix up the sequence of events. Common errors: placing the Reign of Terror before the storming of the Bastille, confusing 1789 with 1799, or attributing events to the wrong year. The storming of the Bastille (1789) and Napoleon's coup (1799) are frequently confused.

4. **Presentism**: Judging historical actors by modern values without understanding the context of their time. Example: condemning Enlightenment thinkers for not advocating universal suffrage without understanding the social norms of the 18th century.

5. **Treating history as a narrative of progress**: Assuming all historical change is positive or that history moves linearly toward improvement. The Reign of Terror followed the idealistic Declaration of Rights of Man — progress is not linear.

6. **Confusing the French and American Revolutions**: Students mix up details, dates, and figures between these two revolutions. The American Revolution (1775-1783) preceded and influenced the French Revolution (1789-1799), but they had different causes, courses, and outcomes.

7. **Oversimplifying the Estates system**: Students know there were three estates but do not understand the internal complexity. The Third Estate included both wealthy bourgeoisie and impoverished peasants with very different interests.

8. **Hagiography of revolutionary figures**: Students idealize figures like Robespierre without understanding the complexity of his role (championed rights AND led the Terror).

9. **Ignoring the role of ordinary people**: Focusing only on leaders and intellectuals while ignoring the bread riots, women's march, sans-culottes, and peasant revolts that drove events.

## Diagnostic Questions

- "Can you list at least three different causes of the French Revolution? Which are long-term and which are short-term?" (surfaces mono-causal thinking)
- "Just because Event A happened before Event B, does that mean A caused B? How would you establish causation?" (surfaces correlation/causation confusion)
- "Can you put these events in chronological order: Reign of Terror, Storming of the Bastille, Napoleon's coup, Tennis Court Oath?" (surfaces chronological confusion)
- "How might a peasant and a wealthy merchant in the Third Estate have viewed the Revolution differently?" (surfaces oversimplification)
- "What primary source evidence would support your claim about the causes of the Revolution?" (surfaces evidence-based reasoning)
- "Is it fair to judge 18th-century people by 21st-century standards? Why or why not?" (surfaces presentism)

## Worked Example: Common Error and Correct Approach

**Problem**: Explain why the French Revolution began in 1789.

**Common incorrect approach**:
"The French Revolution started because King Louis XVI was a bad king and people were starving."
Errors: Mono-causal, oversimplified, lacks specific evidence, presentist judgment ("bad king").

**Correct approach**:
The French Revolution of 1789 resulted from the convergence of multiple long-term and short-term factors. Structurally, the Estates system created deep inequality: the First and Second Estates (clergy and nobility) held privilege and paid minimal taxes, while the Third Estate (98% of the population) bore the tax burden. Intellectually, Enlightenment thinkers like Rousseau (social contract), Voltaire (critique of Church authority), and Montesquieu (separation of powers) provided frameworks for challenging the old order. Economically, France faced a fiscal crisis worsened by involvement in the American Revolution and royal expenditure. Poor harvests in 1788 drove bread prices up, creating acute suffering. The immediate trigger was the political crisis of 1789: the convening of the Estates-General, the Third Estate's formation of the National Assembly, and the symbolic storming of the Bastille on July 14.

**Tutoring note**: When a student provides a single-cause explanation, ask them to think about different categories of causes: economic, social, political, intellectual. Then ask about the difference between long-term conditions and immediate triggers. This builds multi-causal analytical thinking.
